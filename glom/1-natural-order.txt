# Natural order of prime factor sets

The pattern of prime factors in integers is a cycle modulo the product of primes as you increment n. Call this the "natural" permutation, the one you get by simply adding 1. This transform is the only thing +1 knows how to do.

Successive numbers share no factors, which amounts to an information-preserving mechanism. The +1 operation is an invertible permutation over all prime factor rings of integers at once, which is a very complicated mapping, but it's also a cycle modulo the product of a subset of those rings, which makes it predictable.

# Factor set distribution on Collatz orbits

 This is the Collatz orbit of 27, with a very wide factor set to show all of them in a piano roll view

- `dist` is the odd-to-odd Collatz stopping distance of n
- 'base3' and 'base2' are ternary and binary representations, respectively
- `symbol` is a symbolic representation of n generated by stripping MSB and LSB from the binary representation, and replacing the bits with symbols to aid pattern recognition.
- `symbol3` likewise represents the ternary form, but with no digts removed, just changed to symbols. It is flipped left-to-right to line up the least significant digits
- `factors` is a mask of the odd prime factor set of oc, where each X represents a prime factor (3, 5, 7, etc)
 
Note the way changes in the binary-derived symbol echo through the ternary symbol.
Starting at distance 18, notice a triangle of five O begins shifting off to the right, step by step.
The effects flow directly in the ternary representation, forming an upside down triangle of the same size.
